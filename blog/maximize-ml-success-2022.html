How to maximize probability of success for your Machine Learning solution?

Tips and tricks from 15 years as a data scientist

Published on: 20 December 2022

Throughout my 15 years as data scientist in academia, big pharma and through consulting, one common theme has emerged: the most reliable predictor of success for any NLP or ML-based solution is whether or not you involve the data science team early on. By introducing your data scientists to the domain experts right from the start of the project, you can iteratively refine and improve both your data and your ML models.

The Critical Success Factor: Early Collaboration

After working across diverse industries and research environments, one pattern emerges consistently: projects succeed when data scientists and domain experts collaborate from day one, not when data science is brought in as an afterthought to "make the data work."

"The most reliable predictor of ML project success isn't the algorithm you choose or the size of your dataset—it's how early you involve your data science team in the project planning process."

Why Early Involvement Matters

1. Realistic Expectation Setting

Data scientists can assess the feasibility of proposed solutions early, preventing months of development on impossible targets. We help stakeholders understand:

  • What accuracy levels are achievable with available data
  • How much labeled data will be needed for reliable results
  • Which aspects of the problem are inherently difficult to automate
  • What the cost-benefit analysis looks like for different approaches

2. Data Strategy Optimization

When involved from the start, data scientists can:

  • Design data collection processes that will actually support the intended use case
  • Identify critical data quality issues before they become expensive problems
  • Establish annotation guidelines that lead to consistent, useful labels
  • Plan for the long-term maintenance and updating of data assets

3. Iterative Refinement

Early collaboration enables continuous improvement cycles:

  • Quick prototypes validate assumptions before major investments
  • Regular feedback sessions align technical development with business needs
  • Small-scale tests identify issues while they're still easy to fix
  • Domain expertise informs model architecture and feature engineering

Common Anti-Patterns to Avoid

The "Clean Up Our Data" Trap

Scenario: "We have all this data, we just need someone to clean it up and build a model."

Reality: Without understanding the intended use case, data scientists waste time on irrelevant cleaning tasks while missing critical quality issues.

The "Magic Algorithm" Myth

Scenario: "We need 99% accuracy. Find the right algorithm to get us there."

Reality: Accuracy requirements should be based on data quality and business impact, not arbitrary targets.

The "Build First, Integrate Later" Problem

Scenario: "Build the model in isolation, then we'll figure out how to integrate it."

Reality: Integration requirements fundamentally affect model design, performance requirements, and success metrics.

Practical Steps for Success

Week 1: Joint Discovery

  • Data scientists and domain experts workshop the problem together
  • Map out the current process and identify automation opportunities
  • Define success metrics that matter to the business
  • Assess data availability and quality constraints

Week 2-3: Rapid Prototyping

  • Build simple baseline models with existing data
  • Identify gaps between current performance and requirements
  • Test key assumptions about data quality and model complexity
  • Create shared understanding of what "good enough" looks like

Ongoing: Collaborative Iteration

  • Weekly check-ins to review progress and adjust priorities
  • Joint review of model outputs and failure cases
  • Continuous refinement of annotation guidelines and data collection
  • Regular reassessment of business requirements and success metrics

Lessons from the Field

Pharmaceutical Research

In drug discovery projects, early collaboration between chemists and data scientists led to more relevant molecular descriptors and better prediction models. When domain experts helped design the feature engineering process, model performance improved by 30% compared to purely algorithmic approaches.

Academic Research

In computational biology projects, involving biologists in the annotation scheme design prevented months of rework. Their insights about biological relevance helped prioritize which errors to fix first, leading to models that were both accurate and scientifically meaningful.

Consulting Projects

Across various industries, clients who included data scientists in their initial planning meetings had 3x higher project success rates than those who brought us in after requirements were already fixed.

The Bottom Line

Machine learning isn't magic—it's a collaborative process that requires both technical expertise and deep domain knowledge. The most successful projects recognize this from day one and structure their teams accordingly.

Don't wait until you have "perfect" data or "clear" requirements to involve your data science team. The messiness of real-world data and evolving business needs is exactly why you need that expertise early in the process.

Key Takeaway

Success in machine learning isn't about having the best algorithm or the biggest dataset. It's about having the right conversations at the right time, with the right people in the room. Make sure your data scientists are part of those conversations from the very beginning.
